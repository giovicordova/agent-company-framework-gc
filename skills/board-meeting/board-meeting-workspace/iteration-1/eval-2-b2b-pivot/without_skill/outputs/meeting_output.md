# Board Meeting: Should We Pivot from B2B to B2C for Our SaaS Product?

**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Called by:** Gio
**Attendees:** Strategist, Editor, Recruiter

---

## Strategist

This is a fundamental repositioning question, so I need to run the 3-axis check before anyone gets excited.

**Market size:** B2C markets are larger by volume but brutal on unit economics. Customer acquisition cost in B2C SaaS averages 5-7x higher per dollar of lifetime value compared to B2B. Unless we have a viral or product-led growth loop already validated, we are walking into a margin war.

**Execution feasibility:** A pivot is not a feature toggle. B2B and B2C require different sales motions, different support models, different pricing architectures, and different product surfaces. We would essentially be building a second company. Do we have the runway for that? If current B2B revenue is sustaining us, a full pivot risks the base while chasing unproven demand.

**Defensibility:** B2B contracts create switching costs. Enterprise customers are sticky. B2C users churn at 5-8% monthly in most SaaS categories. Our moat weakens significantly if we move to B2C unless the product has strong network effects or data lock-in.

**My recommendation:** Do not pivot. Instead, explore a B2B2C layer -- let our B2B clients be the distribution channel to end consumers. This preserves the revenue base, tests B2C demand with someone else's acquisition budget, and keeps our moat intact. A full pivot without validated B2C traction data is a high-risk, low-information bet.

---

## Editor

The Strategist covered the numbers. I want to flag the messaging problem.

B2B and B2C speak entirely different languages. Our current brand voice, website copy, onboarding flows, documentation -- all of it is calibrated for a buyer who evaluates ROI, integration specs, and compliance. A B2C user wants to know what the product does for them in five seconds. That is the Scan Test, and our entire content stack would fail it for a consumer audience.

Rebuilding the messaging layer is not trivial. It means new positioning, new tone, new content strategy, new onboarding copy, potentially a new brand identity. That is months of work and it competes directly with product development for attention.

If we do pursue B2C in any form -- even the B2B2C route the Strategist mentioned -- we need a dedicated content track for it. Bolting consumer messaging onto enterprise copy never works. It confuses both audiences and dilutes the brand.

**My recommendation:** Before any pivot decision, validate whether we can even articulate a B2C value proposition that passes the Scan Test. If we cannot explain what the product does for a consumer in one sentence, we are not ready for B2C regardless of strategy.

---

## Recruiter

I need to raise the human element here because both the Strategist and Editor are describing what is effectively a different company, and that means a different team.

Running a quick calibration protocol on our current team composition: our people are hired and structured for B2B. That means account management skills, enterprise sales experience, technical integration support. B2C requires growth marketers, product designers obsessed with consumer UX, community managers, and a support team built for volume, not depth.

The skill gap is not something you bridge with training. It is a fundamentally different talent profile. A pivot means either:

- Hiring an entirely new layer of B2C-specialist roles (expensive, slow, dilutes culture)
- Replacing existing B2B talent with B2C talent (destructive and demoralising if B2B was working)
- Asking current staff to operate outside their domain (sets them up to fail)

None of those options are clean.

**My recommendation:** If B2C is on the table in any form, start with a small dedicated squad -- two or three people max -- running it as an experiment alongside the core B2B operation. Do not restructure the whole team around an unvalidated hypothesis. The people cost of a failed pivot is the hardest to recover from.

---

## Board Consensus

All three agents advise against a full pivot. The converging recommendation:

1. **Do not abandon B2B.** It is the proven revenue base with built-in defensibility.
2. **Test B2C through a B2B2C channel** or a small isolated experiment before committing resources.
3. **Validate the consumer value proposition first.** If we cannot pass the Scan Test for a B2C audience, the product is not ready.
4. **Do not restructure the team.** Run any B2C exploration with a small, dedicated squad.
5. **Set a kill criteria.** Define what B2C traction looks like (specific metrics, timeline) and commit to walking away if it does not materialise.

**Decision required from Gio:** Proceed with B2B2C exploration, or table B2C entirely and double down on the current B2B position.
