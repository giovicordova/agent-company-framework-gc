# Board Meeting — Hiring a Part-Time Content Writer

**Date:** 2026-04-12
**Attendees:** Strategist, Editor, Recruiter
**Agenda:** Define requirements and positioning for a part-time content writer role.

---

## Strategist

The first question is what this role actually produces in terms of revenue leverage. A content writer only makes sense if we have a clear content-to-pipeline strategy — meaning every piece they write should either attract leads, nurture prospects, or reinforce positioning.

**3-axis check:**

- **Market size:** Content is a proven channel, but part-time output limits volume. We need someone who can produce high-signal pieces, not filler. Quality over quantity.
- **Execution feasibility:** Part-time is realistic if scope is tight — 2 to 3 deliverables per week maximum. More than that and quality drops or the role creeps into full-time hours.
- **Defensibility:** Content builds a compounding moat if it is genuinely useful to our audience. Generic content does not. The writer must understand our domain well enough to produce material competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Positioning recommendation:** Frame this as a "Content Strategist (Part-Time)" rather than "Content Writer." The word "strategist" attracts candidates who think about why they write, not just what. It also sets the expectation that they will contribute to editorial direction, not just execute briefs.

**Risk flag:** If we position it as a basic writing gig, we will attract generalists who need heavy editing. That costs more in Editor time than it saves.

---

## Editor

Strategist is right about the title. "Writer" attracts volume applicants. "Content Strategist" filters for people who care about outcomes.

**What I need from this person:**

- They must pass the Scan Test on their own work. If their portfolio is full of meandering, front-loaded intros, they are not the right fit. I want someone whose writing gets to the point within the first line.
- Brand voice alignment. They need to absorb our tone — direct, no jargon, no filler — and produce work that sounds like us, not like a generic content mill.
- Self-editing ability. Part-time means I cannot review every draft in real time. They need to ship clean copy with minimal rounds of revision.

**What to look for in the portfolio:**

- Short-form pieces that demonstrate clarity under constraint (newsletters, product updates, landing pages).
- Evidence they have written for a specific audience, not "everyone."
- Bonus: experience writing about technical subjects for non-technical readers — that mirrors our communication style exactly.

**What to avoid:**

- Portfolios padded with 2,000-word SEO articles that say nothing. Length is not skill.
- Candidates who describe themselves as "passionate storytellers." We need a communicator, not a narrator.

---

## Recruiter

Both points land. Let me translate this into a hiring structure.

**Calibration protocol — adjusted weights for this role:**

| Criterion | Weight | What we are measuring |
|---|---|---|
| Writing skill | 45% | Clarity, economy, Scan Test pass rate on portfolio samples |
| Domain relevance | 25% | Have they written in our space or adjacent spaces? Can they learn fast? |
| Culture alignment | 20% | Self-directed, low-maintenance, comfortable with async feedback loops |
| Growth potential | 10% | Could this scale to full-time or broader content ownership later? |

**Screening process:**

1. **Portfolio review** — 3 samples minimum. Editor scores each against the Scan Test before we speak to anyone.
2. **Paid trial task** — a real brief from our pipeline, not a hypothetical. We pay for the trial regardless of outcome. This filters for quality and shows we respect their time.
3. **Async interview** — written Q&A rather than a live call. Part-time content people are often juggling multiple clients. A written format lets them show their communication style naturally.
4. **Editor review of trial output** — does it pass the Scan Test? Does it sound like us? Does it need heavy revision?

**Positioning the role externally:**

- Lead with flexibility and autonomy — that is what part-time candidates value most.
- Be specific about volume: "2 to 3 pieces per week, async workflow, minimal meetings."
- State the tone expectation upfront: "We write short, direct, jargon-free. If that is your style, we want to hear from you."
- Mention the paid trial in the listing. It signals professionalism and filters out candidates who are not serious.

**Compensation note:** Part-time content roles are typically per-piece or per-hour. Per-piece aligns incentives better — it rewards efficiency and discourages padding. Suggest we benchmark rates against current market for our content type and set a clear per-piece range in the listing.

---

## Consensus

All three agents agree on the following:

- **Title:** Content Strategist (Part-Time), not "Content Writer."
- **Core filter:** The Scan Test applied to portfolio and trial output. If the writing does not pass, the candidate does not advance.
- **Process:** Portfolio review, paid trial task, async written interview, Editor sign-off.
- **Positioning:** Lead with autonomy, async workflow, clear volume expectations, and paid trial. Avoid generic job-board language.
- **Compensation model:** Per-piece preferred over hourly to align output quality with pay.
